Don Norman's Interface Design Principles

Don Norman suggests organizing screens and menus functionally, designing commands and menu choices to be distinctive, and making it difficult for users to take irreversible actions. Norman also says to provide feedback about the state of the interface (e.g., changing the cursor to show whether a map interface is in zoom-in or select mode) and designing for consistency of actions (e.g., ensuring that Yes/No buttons are always displayed in the same order). Difference Among Guidelines, Principles, and Theories

Guidelines Specific recommendations or instructions for designing interfaces.
: Specific recommendations or instructions for designing interfaces. Guidelines provide practical advice on how to achieve certain design goals. For example, a guideline could recommend using clear and concise language in interface text, or to provide advanced usability functions like undo, copy, paste, or duplicate for professional users.
Principles Fundamental concepts or philosophies that guide design decisions.
: Fundamental concepts or philosophies that guide design decisions. They provide paramount themes or ideals that designers should strive to achieve. An example of a principle is "consistency," which suggests that interface elements should behave predictably throughout an application.
Theories Frameworks or models that explain cognitive, psychological, and behavioral aspects of user interaction.
: Frameworks or models that explain the cognitive, psychological, and behavioral aspects of user interaction with technology. They provide a deeper understanding of how users interact with interfaces and can inform design practices. An example of an HCI theory is "Fitts' Law," which describes the relationship between target size, distance, and user interaction time.

Accessibility Guidelines and Progress

Accessibility guidelines are standards and recommendations aimed at making digital products and services usable by people with disabilities. Organizations such as the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) and the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) lead efforts in developing and promoting accessibility guidelines.

Examples of Accessibility Guidelines:

  • Providing alternative text for images to assist users who are visually impaired.
  • Ensuring keyboard navigation is possible for users who cannot use a mouse.
  • Designing with sufficient color contrast to accommodate users with low vision.

Progress in accessibility guidelines includes updates to existing standards, increased awareness and adoption of accessibility practices, and efforts to make digital content more inclusive and accessible to all users.

Difference Between Micro-HCI Theories and Macro-HCI Theories

Micro-HCI Theories Focus on specific elements or aspects of human-computer interaction.
: These theories focus on specific elements or aspects of human-computer interaction, such as the design of individual interface components like buttons, icons, or menus. Micro-HCI theories address detailed usability issues and design considerations at object level.
Macro-HCI Theories Examine broader aspects of human-computer interaction.
: Macro-HCI theories examine broader aspects of human-computer interaction, such as overall system design, user workflows, and the overall user experience. These theories consider how users interact with entire applications or systems and how design choices impact usability and user satisfaction on a larger scale.
